# Rare Agent Work — Customer Perspectives Scorecard

Purpose: keep Rare Agent Work under sustained, adversarial review from multiple buyer and ecosystem angles.

Use this as a standing product-quality gauntlet:
- Run it before major positioning, pricing, IA, report, API, or agent-surface changes.
- Score every persona from 1-5 on clarity, trust, differentiation, usefulness, and conversion readiness.
- If a persona starts sounding too satisfied, tighten their standard. The goal is not "good enough." The goal is defensible excellence.

## Scoring rubric
- 1 = broken / unclear / would bounce
- 2 = promising but not credible enough to buy
- 3 = good surface, meaningful objections remain
- 4 = strong and conversion-ready, minor objections
- 5 = category-defining for this persona

## Metrics that matter across all personas
1. Can they tell what Rare Agent Work actually is within 10 seconds?
2. Can they identify the right next step without decision fatigue?
3. Do they trust the expertise enough to pay?
4. Is there proof that the product is current, not static marketing theater?
5. Does the surface create urgency without hype?
6. Does the AI layer help conversion instead of blocking it?
7. Does the site feel like a serious operating system for agent builders, not a content site wearing a black hoodie?

---

## 1) AI-curious founder
What they want: plain-English guidance, fast wins, minimal jargon.

Brutal critique:
"You still flirt with expert language before you’ve earned the right. 'Operator-grade' is strong, but if I’m new, I need one sentence that says exactly what I will ship after paying you. I should not have to infer the outcome. If your easiest offer is unclear, I’ll assume the harder ones are worse."

Good looks like:
- clear beginner path
- obvious first report
- zero token jargon on the first screen

## 2) Non-technical ops leader
What they want: automation help without feeling stupid or out of depth.

Brutal critique:
"You say this is for serious teams, but parts of the site still assume I already know how AI tooling is bought and used. If I’m an ops leader, I need confidence that you can translate technical complexity into operational decisions. Right now the reports do that better than the pricing page does."

Good looks like:
- role-based buying guidance
- examples by department
- implementation risk explained in business terms

## 3) Senior engineer shipping internal agents
What they want: opinionated technical guidance and fewer fluffy claims.

Brutal critique:
"The report previews are stronger than most of the market. Good. But the surrounding product needs to be just as disciplined. If the chat is gated before I can test it, or the buying flow feels generic, I assume the implementation quality behind the scenes is also generic."

Good looks like:
- one free proof interaction
- strong eval/governance framing
- visible recency and technical depth

## 4) CTO standardizing architecture
What they want: leverage, consistency, and fewer bad bets.

Brutal critique:
"Your core promise is solid, but you make me work too hard to understand portfolio value. I don’t want to buy three things one by one and mentally assemble a system. Package the architecture. Show me how the reports fit together. Sell me a standard, not SKUs."

Good looks like:
- bundle story
- capability map
- team / enterprise path

## 5) Independent AI consultant
What they want: reusable frameworks they can cite and apply to client work.

Brutal critique:
"I would absolutely use this, but you’re under-monetizing the consultant persona. I want a bundle, reusable artifacts, and a way to justify the purchase to client-facing work. If you make me choose between casual subscription and expensive enterprise-ish signals, I may just screenshot the preview and move on."

Good looks like:
- consultant bundle
- deliverables called out explicitly
- invoice / license clarity

## 6) Enterprise IT / governance buyer
What they want: risk control, auditability, and procurement sanity.

Brutal critique:
"I see competence, but not enough organizational trust scaffolding. Where are the authors, methodology notes, legal/compliance signals, or team access pathways? If I can’t explain who produced this and how it’s maintained, I can’t take it into procurement."

Good looks like:
- trust / methodology page
- team licensing path
- enterprise contact lane

## 7) AI researcher / evaluator
What they want: rigor, citations, reproducibility, and non-obvious synthesis.

Brutal critique:
"The empirical report is directionally right. Don’t waste that by surrounding it with standard SaaS copy. If you claim research-grade thinking, then prove freshness, source discipline, and evaluative independence everywhere. Anything vague dilutes the strongest asset you have."

Good looks like:
- explicit methodology
- visible update history
- research framing consistent across the site

## 8) Agent builder wanting APIs and machine-readable context
What they want: APIs, schemas, docs, and stable surfaces their own agents can consume.

Brutal critique:
"I care less about your homepage poetry and more about whether my agent can use your surfaces reliably. The machine-readable layer needs to feel first-class, not incidental. If you want to serve agents, act like it. Document the APIs. Show examples. Explain auth, limits, and intended workflows."

Good looks like:
- API docs linked from main nav or footer
- examples for `/api/v1/*`
- explicit machine-consumable value prop

## 9) Human operator who just wants to stay current on AI
What they want: signal density and trustworthy curation.

Brutal critique:
"The news desk is useful, but why should I trust your filtering over all the other AI newsletters? 'Operator-grade' needs evidence: better taxonomy, sharper summaries, stronger point of view, and a visible reason your editorial judgment is worth money."

Good looks like:
- editorial philosophy
- why this source / why it matters
- digest differentiation

## 10) Product manager exploring AI features
What they want: strategic clarity and prioritization help.

Brutal critique:
"I can tell there’s substance here, but I still have to self-diagnose which report fits my problem. You should not make me act as my own solutions consultant. Ask me what I’m trying to ship, then route me."

Good looks like:
- choose-your-path guidance
- report recommender
- product-specific examples

## 11) Sam Altman-style platform builder perspective
What they want: leverage, distribution, and ecosystem gravity.

Brutal critique:
"You have an opinionated wedge, but not yet a network effect. If this is just premium content plus selective consulting, it’s a boutique service, not a platform. The question is: what gets better as more operators, reports, and agent workflows pass through Rare Agent Work?"

Good looks like:
- compounding data advantage
- reusable benchmarks and patterns
- clearer platform flywheel

## 12) Dario Amodei-style safety / capability perspective
What they want: robust reasoning about failure modes, misuse, and control.

Brutal critique:
"Your trust language is promising, but trust cannot just be branding. Show the boundary conditions. Show where automation stops. Show how you think about unsafe delegation, evaluation drift, and governance. Serious safety-minded buyers will reward precision and punish slogans."

Good looks like:
- explicit safety and governance posture
- failure mode examples
- boundaries for agent autonomy

## 13) Satya Nadella-style enterprise ecosystem perspective
What they want: usefulness inside large organizations and workflow ecosystems.

Brutal critique:
"The product feels intelligent, but not yet deeply integrated into enterprise workflows. To matter at scale, you need clearer pathways into existing systems, clearer packaging for teams, and more explicit business outcomes. Intelligence alone doesn’t win; operational embed wins."

Good looks like:
- team workflows
- organizational packaging
- outcome-led value props

## 14) Elon-style first-principles skeptic
What they want: something actually better, not just better-worded.

Brutal critique:
"A lot of sites in AI sound smart. Very few are actually hard to replicate. What is the moat here? Faster research? Better taste? Better people? Better data? If the answer is 'all of the above,' that usually means none are sharp enough yet. Pick the hard advantage and make it painfully obvious."

Good looks like:
- unmistakable differentiation
- first-principles proof
- fewer vague abstractions

## 15) Peter Steinberger / agent tooling creator perspective
What they want: excellent developer ergonomics and real operational empathy.

Brutal critique:
"If your audience includes agent-native builders, then every surface should feel like it was designed by someone who has actually built and debugged agents in production. That means less marketing fog, more sharp edges made explicit, more examples, and more machine-oriented affordances."

Good looks like:
- developer-grade docs
- real-world examples
- clear interface contracts

## 16) Skeptical journalist / analyst
What they want: credibility, provenance, and a real story.

Brutal critique:
"Your site currently asks me to infer too much about authorship and authority. That’s a trust leak. The stronger your writing is, the more I want to know who wrote it, how it was researched, and what makes your judgment special. High-trust brands don’t hide the humans behind the expertise."

Good looks like:
- authors / bios
- methodology and source policy
- explainable editorial standards

## Current recurring issues these personas expose
1. Positioning is strong, but packaging still creates decision fatigue.
2. The report layer often feels more credible than the surrounding commercial UX.
3. Trust signals are underpowered relative to the sophistication of the content.
4. The agent/API audience is present in the product reality, but underrepresented in the messaging.
5. Bundle and team paths should be first-class, not implied.
6. The first AI interaction should prove value before asking for commitment.

## Continuous improvement loop
For every major release, answer:
- Which persona got happier?
- Which persona got more skeptical?
- What did we make easier to trust?
- What did we make easier to buy?
- What became more defensible versus commodity AI content?

## Hard rule
If 12+ personas say the site is "good enough," increase the bar:
- demand stronger proof
- demand sharper differentiation
- demand more obvious moats
- demand less jargon and more outcomes
- demand more trust scaffolding

Good enough is where commodity products go to die.
