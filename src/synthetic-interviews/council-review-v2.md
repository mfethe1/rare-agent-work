# Rare Agent Work — Continuous Improvement Council v2

16 deliberately harsh perspectives reviewing the live product as if they had used the site, reports, ask surface, and public APIs.

## How to use this

- Treat this as a standing quality bar, not a one-off brainstorm.
- Re-run the council after every meaningful product change.
- Track scores by persona and dimension, but pay more attention to repeated objections.
- When a complaint is fixed, increase the panel's proof standard. Good is not the goal. Category-defining is.

---

## 1) Solo Founder — wants to stay up to date on AI without becoming an AI hobbyist

> "I can tell this is smarter than the average AI site, but I still have to work too hard to understand what I should buy first. 'Operator-grade' is strong positioning, but it is still positioning. Show me the exact outcome: what gets easier for me this week if I trust you? Right now the site sounds premium before it proves premium."

Brutal critique:
- Value prop is still too self-referential.
- The site explains what Rare Agent Work is, not what it changes for me by Friday.
- Pricing makes sense after reading carefully, not at a glance.

Would convert if:
- There is a blunt "start here if you are non-technical" path.
- The first report promises a concrete before/after outcome.

## 2) AI-Curious Operator — follows AI daily and wants filtered signal

> "The news desk is directionally right, but I still don't know why your filtering is better than five good X accounts and a Perplexity habit. If you're the trusted filter, prove it with sharper editorial judgment, stronger operator takeaways, and visible standards. Otherwise this is curation theater with nicer typography."

Brutal critique:
- News feels useful, but not yet indispensable.
- There is not enough obvious editorial signature per story.
- "Why this matters" needs more bite and more contrarian filtering.

Would convert if:
- Each story had a stronger operator action note and confidence level.
- The feed showed what you intentionally ignored and why.

## 3) Staff Engineer — evaluating tools, APIs, and reliability claims

> "The public API exists, which is already better than most content sites pretending to be agent-friendly. But the product still has a trust gap: I see claims about agent compatibility, not enough evidence about stability, rate limits, versioning, response semantics, and maintenance discipline. If you want agent builders to rely on this, stop sounding helpful and start sounding dependable."

Brutal critique:
- Agent-friendliness is present, but not production-grade.
- Docs are discoverable, but operational guarantees are thin.
- I can't tell how much I should trust this in a real workflow.

Would convert if:
- OpenAPI had explicit examples, update policy, and deprecation rules.
- API docs clearly separated stable versus experimental surfaces.

## 4) Autonomous Agent Client — wants machine-usable context with minimal ambiguity

> "You say you are agent-friendly, but you still leak human marketing language into places agents consume. That increases ambiguity. Your ask endpoint is promising, but I want richer metadata, better provenance, more structured summaries, and clearer freshness semantics. If my orchestration has to guess, your API is incomplete."

Brutal critique:
- The machine surface is useful but under-specified.
- Freshness, provenance, and confidence should be first-class fields everywhere.
- Ask responses need stronger structured grounding, not just nice natural language.

Would convert if:
- Every answer returned citations, timestamps, and source classes consistently.
- Report metadata exposed audience, prerequisites, and depth level in structured form.

## 5) Agent Builder — using your APIs to improve another AI system

> "I can use the API, but I still have to reverse-engineer too much. I want composability. Give me stable report taxonomies, canonical tags, implementation patterns, and cleaner machine paths from question to cited answer to source object. Right now it is agent-friendly in the way a nice website is agent-friendly, not in the way infrastructure is agent-friendly."

Brutal critique:
- Great start, weak developer ergonomics.
- There is no obvious cookbook for orchestration use cases.
- The API looks readable, not deeply integrable.

Would convert if:
- There were copy-paste recipes for retrieval, digesting, report recommendation, and news triage.
- I could depend on consistent schema names across endpoints.

## 6) Independent Consultant — wants authority and reusable client assets

> "The reports look sellable, but the packaging is still optimized for a direct buyer, not for me as a multiplier. I want bundle pricing, team licensing, client-safe export rights, and a clearer reason why your material is more credible than a long memo I could ask a frontier model to draft."

Brutal critique:
- Commercial packaging leaves money on the table.
- Strong content, weak consultant monetization path.

Would convert if:
- You introduced a consultant bundle and reuse license.

## 8) AI Research Lead — evaluating whether the reports are genuinely differentiated

> "Some of the writing sounds more serious than most AI content, which is good. But serious tone is not serious methodology. I need to know what is original here: datasets, comparisons, synthesis process, expert interviews, deployment evidence, or field observations. If you want to be premium, prove epistemic discipline, not just taste."

Brutal critique:
- Differentiation is plausible, not yet undeniable.
- Report authority depends too much on style and too little on visible method.
- I need stronger source and methodology disclosure.

Would convert if:
- Each report had a methodology box: source types, date window, confidence caveats.
- You published a short editorial standard for operator research.

## 9) Skeptical Journalist — trying to verify whether this is real or hype

> "The site reads better than a lot of AI consulting fluff, but the trust stack is incomplete. Where are the bylines, credentials, fieldwork notes, or even a crisp explanation of who Michael is to make these calls? If you want press-level trust, attribution cannot be optional."

Brutal critique:
- Credibility is bottlenecked by missing human attribution.
- The product feels curated by someone real but presented like a faceless brand.

Would convert if:
- There were bylines, backgrounds, and methodology notes.
- Selected pieces included links to primary sources or quoted expert inputs.

## 11) Elon-style First-Principles Operator — ruthless about technical uniqueness

> "Cut the adjectives by half. If the product is good, it should survive plain language. What's the non-obvious thing this does better than just asking the best model directly? If the answer is trust and curation, then compress the site until that is obvious in 10 seconds. If the answer is deeper, make it painfully concrete."

Brutal critique:

- The product demonstrated speed, specificity, and a brutally simple wedge.

## 16) Chief Information Security Officer — default assumption: somebody will misuse this

> "I appreciate the repeated trust-boundary language. But repeated trust-boundary language without visible controls reads like marketing. Where are the logs, review gates, data handling rules, retention periods, incident path, and scoping limitations? If you want security-sensitive buyers, the default should be 'show me the controls.'"

Brutal critique:

- High-trust positioning invites a higher burden of proof.

# Repeated failure themes across the council

## Tier 1 — most repeated and most damaging

1. Trust is claimed faster than it is proven.
2. Differentiation is plausible but not yet unmistakable.
3. Agent-friendly surfaces exist but are not yet elite-level developer products.
4. The business model is visible, but the compounding loop is not.
5. Enterprise and consultant monetization paths are underpackaged.

## Tier 2 — meaningful but fixable quickly

6. The homepage still uses too much premium language relative to proof.
7. Report selection and pricing still create unnecessary cognitive load.
8. News needs stronger editorial signature and operator takeaways.
9. The site needs clearer human attribution and research methodology.
10. Workflow embed and integration stories are too quiet.

# Continuous-improvement scorecard

Use 1-10 scoring for each dimension below. A 7 means good. An 8 means strong. A 9 requires category leadership evidence. A 10 should be basically unreachable.

- Clarity of value proposition
- Trust and credibility
- Proof and differentiation
- API and agent usability
- Report depth and originality
- News utility and editorial sharpness
- Conversion friction
- Enterprise readiness
- Workflow embed potential
- Repeat-visit inevitability
